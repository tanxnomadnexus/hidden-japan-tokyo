Japan Lighthouse Guide: 7 Hidden Coastal Gems Along Honshu Worth Visiting

Honshu's most scenic and least-visited lighthouses — from Chiba's 1874 Inubosaki to Shimane's tallest stone tower. All reachable without a car.

  • Zh-Tw

Japan doesn’t market its lighthouses the way it markets its temples or its cherry blossoms, which is a shame — and also your advantage. Honshu’s coastline stretches for thousands of kilometers, wrapping around sea cliffs, volcanic headlands, and fishing coves that most foreign visitors never see. The lighthouses that stand along it tend to be old, well-built, and set against views that would stop anyone in their tracks.

Below are seven of the finest lighthouse stops along Honshu, chosen for scenery, accessibility, and the general absence of tour groups.

1. Inubosaki Lighthouse, Chiba#

Standing on a basalt cliff above the Pacific, Inubosaki is one of Japan’s oldest Western-style lighthouses, built in 1874 with a design brought in from Britain. It’s also one of only sixteen lighthouses in Japan open to the public for climbing. From the top, on a clear day, you can see Mount Fuji to the west and the Pacific stretching endlessly to the east — a combination that’s hard to beat.

The surrounding cape has walking paths, a small maritime museum, and a coastline of dramatic black rock that looks particularly striking at sunrise. The town of Choshi nearby is one of Japan’s most important fishing ports, with excellent fresh seafood at the harborside market.

Getting there: From Tokyo’s Akihabara Station, take the JR Sobu Line to Chiba, then the Choshi Electric Railway to Inubosaki Station. Total journey about 2 hours.

Best season: Year-round. Winter mornings offer the clearest views of Fuji from the top.

2. Shionomisaki Lighthouse, Wakayama#

Shionomisaki sits at the southernmost tip of Honshu — the very end of the Kii Peninsula — and the feeling of standing there is genuinely dramatic. The lighthouse itself is a clean white tower dating from 1873, set above cliffs that drop directly into the Kuroshio Current. The surrounding cape is covered in subtropical vegetation, and the contrast between the white tower, blue sea, and dark green headland is striking in any season.

This is remote by Honshu standards — getting here takes commitment — but the Kii Peninsula as a whole is one of Japan’s most undervisited regions, home to ancient pilgrimage routes, deep-forest temples, and coastline that feels genuinely wild.

Getting there: From Osaka or Nagoya, take the JR Kisei Line to Kushimoto Station, then a local bus or taxi to the cape (about 20 minutes). Total from Osaka approximately 3 hours.

Best season: May and October for clear skies and comfortable temperatures. Summer can be hot and humid.

3. Kyogamisaki Lighthouse, Kyoto Prefecture#

Most people don’t think of Kyoto as a coastal prefecture, but it has a rugged stretch of Sea of Japan coastline that’s almost entirely off the tourist map. Kyogamisaki Lighthouse stands at the northernmost tip of the Tango Peninsula, on cliffs above churning sea that feels nothing like the manicured gardens of Kyoto City. The lighthouse dates from 1898 and is surrounded by a walking trail through coastal forest.

The Tango Peninsula itself is worth a longer visit — it has quiet fishing villages, excellent seafood (particularly crab in winter), and an unhurried pace that’s rare this close to one of Japan’s most-visited cities.

Getting there: From Kyoto Station, take the Kintetsu Kyoto Line to Nijo, then the Sanin Line to Amanohashidate, then a local bus toward Kyogamisaki. Allow about 3 hours total.

Best season: Winter for dramatic seas and crab season; summer for calmer waters and green coastal hills.

4. Tsurugisaki Lighthouse, Kanagawa#

For a lighthouse within easy reach of Tokyo that still manages to feel genuinely remote, Tsurugisaki on the Miura Peninsula is the answer. The cape sits at the entrance to Tokyo Bay, and the lighthouse — built in 1871, another early Meiji-era structure — looks out over some of the busiest shipping lanes in the world. In the foreground: sea, rocks, and a walking path. In the background: container ships the size of apartment buildings.

The Miura Peninsula around it is good for a half-day of exploration — there are small fishing ports, beach seafood restaurants, and the kind of lived-in coastal atmosphere that’s disappeared from most of Japan’s more developed shorelines.

Getting there: From Shinagawa Station, take the Keikyu Line to Misakiguchi, then a local bus to Tsurugisaki. About 90 minutes total.

Best season: Spring and autumn. Clear winter days offer views across the bay to Mount Fuji.

5. Irozaki Lighthouse, Shizuoka (Izu Peninsula)#

The Izu Peninsula juts into the Pacific south of Tokyo, and its southern tip — Cape Irozaki — is the kind of place that makes you forget you’re still in the greater Tokyo travel orbit. The lighthouse here is white and compact, sitting above a coastline of black lava rock and subtropical scrub. Dolphins are frequently spotted offshore, and the water is clear enough to snorkel in summer.

The cape itself has a botanical garden specializing in aloe plants, which bloom yellow in January — an unexpected sight that draws a quiet crowd of Japanese visitors and almost no one else.

Getting there: From Tokyo, take the Shinkansen to Mishima or Atami, then the Izu Kyuko Line to Shimoda, then a local bus to Irozaki. About 2.5 to 3 hours total.

Best season: January for the aloe bloom; May and October for clear skies and calm seas.

6. Nyudozaki Lighthouse, Akita#

On the Sea of Japan coast of Akita Prefecture, Nyudozaki stands on a low headland covered in wind-bent pine trees, with views north toward the mountains of Aomori and south along a coastline that sees very few foreign visitors. The lighthouse is striped in black and white — unusual for Japan — and the surrounding cape has a small museum and walking paths along the cliff edge.

The area is quietest in autumn, when the pine forest turns golden and the Sea of Japan starts building toward its winter swells. In summer it’s a popular spot for Japanese day-trippers from Akita City, but even then it’s rarely crowded by any reasonable measure.

Getting there: From Akita Station, take a local bus toward Nyudozaki (about 90 minutes). Alternatively, rent a car from Akita for more flexibility along the coast.

Best season: Late September to early November for autumn color and dramatic skies.

7. Hinomisaki Lighthouse, Shimane#

Near the ancient grand shrine of Izumo Taisha, Hinomisaki Lighthouse is the tallest stone lighthouse in Japan — 43.65 meters of white granite rising above a coastline of sea stacks and small islands. Built in 1903, it’s open for climbing, and the view from the top encompasses the Oki Islands on clear days, floating blue shapes in the distance that look barely real.

The surrounding area includes a small shrine, a fish market, and walking paths along the cliffs. Combined with a visit to Izumo Taisha (one of Japan’s oldest and most important shrines, just 10 kilometers away), this makes for an excellent full-day itinerary in one of Japan’s most underexplored prefectures.

Getting there: From Izumo Taisha-mae Station (reached by local rail from Matsue or Izumo City), take a local bus to Hinomisaki. About 20 minutes from the shrine.

Best season: May and June for clear weather; autumn for dramatic skies and fewer visitors.

Final Thoughts#

Japan’s lighthouse network is extensive, well-maintained, and almost entirely overlooked by international tourism. The sixteen lighthouses open for public climbing are worth seeking out specifically — they’re listed by the Japan Lighthouse Association (灯台の日 events in February mark the anniversary of the first Japanese lighthouse).

None of the seven stops above require a rental car, though having one makes the more remote capes significantly more flexible. Pack lunch where possible — headland cafes are rare, and part of the appeal of these places is that they haven’t been developed for tourism at all.
