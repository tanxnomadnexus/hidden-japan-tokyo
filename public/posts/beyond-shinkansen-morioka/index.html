Beyond the Shinkansen Stop: Morioka

The New York Times called it a top destination in 2023. Most tourists still walk straight through. Here's what they're missing in Morioka.
July 16, 2026

In 2023, the New York Times listed Morioka as one of the 52 places to visit in the world. Not in Japan — in the world. For a few months, the city’s name circulated in travel conversations internationally, and a modest wave of visitors followed.

Then the wave subsided, and Morioka went back to being what it had been before: a city that most foreign tourists pass through on the Tohoku Shinkansen without stopping, on their way to somewhere else.

This is, in some ways, ideal. The NYT attention brought a few more eyes to Morioka without fundamentally changing it. The noodle shops are still full of locals. The ironware studios are still run by the same families. The morning market still operates for the city’s residents first and visitors second.

Here’s what’s actually there.

The basics#

Morioka is about 2 hours 10 minutes from Tokyo on the Tohoku Shinkansen — the same line that continues north to Aomori and, via branch lines, to Akita and Hokkaido. A Japan Rail Pass covers the journey.

The city sits at the confluence of three rivers, with Mt. Iwate — a dormant volcano that looks startlingly like a smaller Mt. Fuji — visible on clear days from almost anywhere in the center. The downtown is compact enough to walk in a single day, though two days lets you do it properly.

One useful fact: Morioka Station is a junction where the Akita Shinkansen branches off from the Tohoku line. If you’re doing a Tohoku loop, Morioka is a natural midpoint — stop for a night or two on your way north or south rather than treating it as a destination that requires special detour.

The three noodles: the only food rule you need#

Morioka has what locals call the “Three Great Noodles,” and understanding them before you arrive is the single most useful thing you can do for your trip.

Wanko soba is the one most visitors have heard of, and it’s genuinely unlike any other eating experience in Japan. You sit at a table, a server stands beside you with a stack of small lacquered bowls, and they keep refilling your bowl with tiny portions of buckwheat noodles — one mouthful at a time — until you put the lid on your bowl to signal you’re done. The record, apparently, is somewhere above 500 bowls. The average visitor manages 50 to 100. It costs around ¥3,000 for the full session, which includes dipping sauces and side dishes.

The competitive element is real — servers call out encouragement, and there’s a tally of how many you’ve eaten — but what stays with you afterward isn’t the number. It’s the particular strangeness of eating a meal at someone else’s pace, in small increments, while being actively cheered on by a stranger. Azumaya Honten (operating since 1907) is the classic choice; Chokurian is a good alternative if you want a slightly calmer atmosphere.

Jajamen is less famous outside Japan and, in my experience, more interesting. Thick, flat wheat noodles arrive topped with a savory miso-meat sauce, cucumber, and ginger. You mix everything together and eat. When you finish the noodles, here’s the part nobody tells you: crack a raw egg into the remaining sauce in the bowl, add a small amount of broth (ask the server — they’ll know what you mean), and you get a completely different dish called chitantan, a thin egg soup made from what was just your noodle bowl. Pairon, the original jajamen shop operating since 1953, is the place to go.

Reimen — cold noodles in chilled beef broth, served with kimchi, cucumber, and sometimes a slice of watermelon — has Korean origins and became a Morioka specialty in the postwar period. It’s best in summer, when the cold broth is particularly welcome, but good year-round. Pyongyang Myeonok is the long-standing local favorite.

My suggestion: don’t try to eat all three in one day. Spread them over two days. Wanko soba for lunch on day one, jajamen for dinner, reimen for lunch on day two. That’s a noodle itinerary, and it’s a good one.

Nanbu Tekki: the ironware you’ll want to carry home#

Nanbu Tekki — cast iron teapots, kettles, wind chimes, and cookware produced in Iwate Prefecture for over 400 years — is probably the best souvenir available anywhere in Tohoku, and Morioka is the best place to buy it.

The craft has a peculiar distinction: Nanbu ironware teapots are used in tea ceremony traditions in a way that ordinary Japanese ironware is not, and the surface texture — a pebbly, matte finish — is immediately recognizable once you know what you’re looking at. A proper tetsubin (iron kettle) changes the water you boil in it, releasing trace minerals that, many people claim, make tea taste noticeably different. Whether or not you believe that, the objects are beautiful and built to last a century.

The practical problem is weight. A full-sized tetsubin can weigh 2 to 3 kilograms. If you’re mid-trip with more destinations ahead, the small tetsubin (around 600ml capacity) or an accessory piece — a small wind chime, a cup, a decorative object — is more practical. Prices range from around ¥2,000 for small pieces to ¥30,000 and above for serious kettles.

Morioka Handi-Works Square (about 20 minutes by bus from the station) has 14 workshops including a Nanbu ironware studio where you can watch artisans at work and, for an additional fee, try casting a small piece yourself. The session takes about 90 minutes and costs around ¥1,500–¥2,000. Worth doing if you have the time — understanding how something is made changes how you look at the finished object.

Downtown, several shops near the Nakatsu River area carry ironware alongside other regional crafts. Kogeisha near the station is a good browsing stop.

The rock-splitting cherry tree#

This takes about ten minutes of your time and is one of those things that’s hard to explain why it’s worth seeing, but it is.

In the middle of Morioka’s downtown, near the Morioka Court of Appeals, a cherry tree grows directly through a large granite boulder. The tree is over 350 years old. The rock has split along the tree’s growth — slowly, over centuries — and the effect is genuinely strange: a substantial tree, blooming normally each spring, emerging from solid stone.

It’s not a garden feature. It’s not landscaped around. It just sits there in a small enclosure, doing what it’s apparently been doing since the 17th century. Free to see, always accessible.

In late April, when it blooms, the combination of cherry blossoms and cracked granite draws a small crowd. At any other time, you might have it to yourself.

Morioka Castle Ruins and Iwate Park#

The castle itself was dismantled during the Meiji era — one of those moments in Japanese history when a lot of feudal-era architecture was torn down in the drive to modernize. What remains are the stone walls and foundations, which are substantial: the quality of the stonework, particularly at the corners where the massive blocks are fitted together without mortar, gives some sense of what the original structure must have looked like.

Iwate Park, which now surrounds the ruins, is pleasant in any season but especially good in spring (cherry blossoms) and autumn (foliage). It’s a 15-minute walk from Morioka Station and free to enter. On weekend afternoons, you’ll see local families, people walking dogs, elderly couples on benches — the park functions as the city’s living room in a way that feels genuinely lived-in.

The Sansa Odori Festival — if you’re here in August#

The Morioka Sansa Odori Festival runs for four evenings in early August and holds a Guinness World Record for the largest taiko drum parade: over 10,000 drummers and dancers moving through the city center, led by floats and accompanied by flute players. The sound, when the procession passes directly in front of you, is physically overwhelming in the best possible way.

What distinguishes Sansa Odori from many Japanese festival crowds is that participation is open. Local companies, schools, and community groups form their own contingents and join the parade. You can sometimes join in the final segment yourself — ask at the tourist information office near the station. The atmosphere is less “spectator event” and more “the whole city is doing this together, and you’re invited.”

If you’re scheduling a Tohoku trip in August, Morioka in early August and Aomori’s Nebuta Festival (also early August, though the dates vary by year) are close enough to combine — two or three days in each, connected by shinkansen in under two hours.

Koiwai Farm — if you have a half-day and want to leave the city#

About 15 kilometers west of Morioka (accessible by bus from the station), Koiwai Farm was established in 1891 and covers approximately 3,000 hectares, making it the largest privately owned farm in Japan. It operates as a kind of open farm and park: you can walk through fields, watch livestock, eat dairy products made on-site, and in spring, see a single famous cherry tree standing alone in a pasture against the backdrop of Mt. Iwate.

The soft-serve ice cream here, made from the farm’s own milk, is better than it has any right to be. This is not something I expected to write, but there it is.

It’s a particularly good option if you’re traveling with children or if you want a half-day of different scenery without a long journey. Entry is around ¥500–¥1,000 depending on the season.

A practical 2-night itinerary#

Day 1

  • Arrive Morioka by shinkansen
  • Afternoon: walk the downtown — Nakatsu River area, Meiji-era red brick buildings, Kogeisha for ironware browsing
  • Rock-splitting cherry tree (on the way)
  • Evening: wanko soba at Azumaya Honten (go at dinner rather than lunch — the competitive energy is better when you’re not rushed)

Day 2

  • Morning: Morioka morning market (Mikoda Morning Market, early start recommended)
  • Mid-morning: Iwate Park and castle ruins
  • Lunch: jajamen at Pairon — don’t forget to make the chitantan afterward
  • Afternoon: bus to Morioka Handi-Works Square for ironware workshop (book ahead)
  • Evening: reimen at Pyongyang Myeonok

Day 3 (optional or transit day)

  • Morning at leisure — café in the Zaimokucho area
  • Depart by shinkansen north to Aomori or south to Sendai/Tokyo

Approximate costs for 2 nights:

  • Business hotel near Morioka Station: ¥7,000–11,000/night
  • Wanko soba session: ¥3,000
  • Jajamen lunch: ¥800–1,200
  • Reimen lunch: ¥900–1,300
  • Ironware workshop: ¥1,500–2,000
  • Food/drink budget: ¥3,000–4,000/day

Total for 2 nights including most of the above: roughly ¥35,000–50,000, not including shinkansen.

When to go#

Late April: The Ishiwarizakura (rock-splitting cherry tree) blooms, and Iwate Park fills with cherry blossoms. One of the better times to be in Morioka, though accommodation books up.

Early August: Sansa Odori Festival. Book everything at least two months ahead.

October–November: Autumn foliage. Koiwai Farm is particularly good in October. Cooler temperatures make walking the downtown more comfortable.

December–March: Cold and occasionally snowy, but the city operates normally and the noodle shops are particularly welcome. Mt. Iwate under snow is worth seeing if you get a clear day.

The New York Times was right, as it turns out. Morioka is worth the stop. It’s a city with a specific, coherent identity — built around craft, noodles, and the particular pride of a regional capital that knows what it is — and two days there leaves you with a clearer sense of how provincial Japan works than almost anything you’ll find closer to Tokyo.
