Japan's Greatest Fireworks Festivals: 4 Shows Worth Planning Your Trip Around

Four of Japan's most spectacular fireworks festivals — where the scale, artistry, and sheer physical impact turn a summer evening into something you won't forget.
June 28, 2026 | Translations:
  • Ko
  • Zh-Tw

Japan has over a thousand fireworks festivals every summer. Most of them are lovely: a warm evening, a river or bay, forty minutes of colour overhead, and everyone cycling home before ten. But a handful of Japanese hanabi events are something categorically different — events where the scale is genuinely overwhelming, the artistry is competitive and taken seriously as a craft, and the crowd of hundreds of thousands of people creates a collective experience that you simply can’t replicate anywhere else.

These aren’t tourist traps. They’re events that Japanese people plan months in advance, queue in ticket lotteries for, and travel across the country to attend. The fact that foreign visitors rarely appear on the riverbanks is partly logistics and partly awareness. This guide exists to fix the second problem; you’ll need to plan well in advance to fix the first.

Here’s what makes each of these festivals worth the effort — and what to actually expect when you arrive.

What Makes a Japanese Fireworks Festival Different#

Before the specific festivals, it’s worth understanding what separates Japanese hanabi culture from the fireworks displays you might be used to.

Japanese fireworks are a craft industry with a history stretching back to the 17th century. The major pyrotechnic families — many of which have been in the business for ten or more generations — treat their work as an art form, not a spectacle. Shells are hand-assembled, compositions are developed and refined over years, and the top festivals are competitive events where companies bring their finest work to be judged against each other.

The shells themselves are different, too. Japan uses spherical shells that burst into perfect circles — the chrysanthemum, the peony, the willow — rather than the asymmetrical bursts common in Western displays. The largest shells, the sanjakudama (36-inch, roughly the size of a washing machine), take over a year to hand-assemble and detonate with a concussive boom that you feel in your chest from half a mile away.

Then there’s the atmosphere around the fireworks. Japanese festival culture builds an entire world around the main event: rows of yatai (food stalls) selling yakitori, takoyaki, grilled corn, kakigori (shaved ice), and cold beer; crowds in yukata (summer kimono) moving slowly through the warm evening; the smell of grilled food mixing with woodsmoke and the sulphur of gunpowder. For foreign visitors, this surrounding culture — the stalls, the clothing, the crowd’s collective reactions — is often as memorable as the fireworks themselves.

1. Omagari National Fireworks Competition, Akita — The Olympics of Japanese Fireworks#

Date 2026: August 29 (Saturday) Location: Omono River Riverbank, Daisen City, Akita Prefecture Scale: Around 18,000 shells, 700,000+ spectators

If you can attend only one fireworks event in Japan, the argument for Omagari is strong. A tradition dating back to 1910, this festival is known throughout Japan as the definitive fireworks competition, where pyrotechnicians from across the country compete to show off their skills. Competitors vie in three categories: daytime fireworks, number-ten round-shell fireworks, and creative fireworks, with the overall champion receiving the Prime Minister’s Prize.

The daytime fireworks are unique — smoke shells that paint the sky in colours against the afternoon light, a category that exists almost nowhere else — and the nighttime competition runs for hours, each company presenting their full program before the climactic cooperative finale. The finale, featuring wide starmines a year in the making from the Omagari Fireworks Cooperative Association, leaves viewers speechless.

Omagari itself is a small town in Daisen City, inland Akita Prefecture. For most of the year, it is a quiet rural community surrounded by rice paddies and mountains. But on the last Saturday of August, over 300,000 people descend on the banks of the Omono River for the National Fireworks Competition.

The festival atmosphere: The approach to the riverside fills with yatai hours before the fireworks begin. Yakitori smoke drifts across the crowd, cold Akita sake is sold in paper cups, and the majority of the crowd arrives in yukata — the sea of patterned cotton under the summer sky, before a single firework has gone up, is its own spectacle. Families spread picnic sheets on the grass; older couples claim their regular spot early; groups of young people arrive loud and excited. By the time the daytime fireworks begin at 5:10pm, the riverbank has transformed into a temporary city.

Practical notes: The venue is the Omagari Fireworks Park on the Yokote Riverbank, approximately 25-40 minutes walk from JR Omagari Station under normal conditions. Book accommodation in the surrounding area months in advance — Omagari itself has limited options, and Akita City (40 minutes by train) fills quickly. Paid reserved seats are sold via lottery in June and July; free standing areas on the riverbanks fill up hours before the event. Plan for the return journey to take two to three hours — the post-show crowd is formidable.

2. Nagaoka Festival Grand Fireworks, Niigata — The Most Emotionally Powerful Show in Japan#

Dates 2026: August 2–3 (Sunday–Monday) Location: Shinano River, Nagaoka City, Niigata Prefecture Scale: 20,000 shells per night, over 400,000 spectators each night

Nagaoka’s fireworks are among the largest in Japan by any measure, but what sets them apart from a simple scale competition is their history. The Nagaoka Festival originated in 1946 as a “Nagaoka Revival Festival” to commemorate the recovery from the Nagaoka air raid on August 1, 1945, when the city was bombed and as much as 80% destroyed, resulting in 1,488 casualties. The festival begins on August 1st with a peace ceremony before the fireworks on the 2nd and 3rd.

The emotional centrepiece of the event is the Phoenix. First launched in 2004 to pray for the recovery from the Niigata Prefecture Chuetsu Earthquake, the Phoenix wide starmine fireworks spread across the sky, accompanied by Ayaka Hirahara’s song “Jupiter,” evoking chills and awe. The Phoenix spans nearly two kilometres across the river — you cannot take in its full width without moving your head — and during the five minutes it runs, the crowd of hundreds of thousands falls completely silent except for occasional sounds of genuine emotion. It is among the most moving things you can witness at a public event in Japan.

The Sho-Sanjakudama shell is the other highlight: first launched in 1926, this historic firework blooms into a massive flower with a diameter of 650 meters in the sky, its slow-motion-like ascent and thunderous sound making for an incredibly powerful experience. 2026 marks the centenary of this shell — expect the event to be particularly significant this year.

The festival atmosphere: Besides fireworks, portable shrine and float parades, folk dance processions, and toro nagashi (floating paper lanterns down the river) are also major events at the festival. The main street from Nagaoka Station becomes a river of yukata-clad festival-goers by late afternoon, with yatai lining the route selling Niigata specialties — rice crackers, local sake from one of the prefecture’s many breweries, fresh seafood from the Japan Sea coast an hour away. The atmosphere along the river before the fireworks is genuinely festive in the old-fashioned sense of the word: communal, warm, celebratory.

Practical notes: Nagaoka is a stop on the Joetsu Shinkansen Line, directly accessible from Tokyo Station in about one hour and 39 minutes. All viewing areas require tickets, sold by lottery through the official website. Since 2022 free viewing has not been available. Book your ticket and accommodation simultaneously — the two nights sell out in parallel. A guided bus tour from Tokyo, which handles both transportation and reserved seating, is the most practical option for international visitors.

3. Tsuchiura National Fireworks Competition, Ibaraki — The Third Major, Often Overlooked#

Date 2026: Late October (exact date TBC — check official site) Location: Sakura River, Tsuchiura City, Ibaraki Prefecture Scale: Around 20,000 shells, 500,000 spectators

The overall winner of the Omagari event receives the prestigious Prime Minister’s Award, which is only given at one other fireworks event: Ibaraki’s Tsuchiura Fireworks Festival. That shared status makes Tsuchiura the third leg of Japan’s fireworks competition triumvirate — and its October timing makes it a practical option for travelers who can’t arrange a summer visit.

The autumn timing also means the fireworks go up in cooler, clearer air, which affects how the shells expand and how far the sound carries. Late October evenings in Ibaraki can be cold by the time the show ends — bring layers — but the visual clarity of autumn fireworks against a dark sky is noticeably different from the humid haze of August.

Tsuchiura is less visited by foreign travelers than Nagaoka or Omagari, which means the surrounding festival atmosphere retains a more local character: the yatai food stalls are catering primarily to the regulars rather than to tourists, and the crowd around the riverbank has the particular energy of a town doing something it does every year and takes quiet pride in.

Practical notes: Tsuchiura is on the JR Joban Line, about 50 minutes from Tokyo’s Ueno Station — making it the most logistically straightforward of the three competition festivals. Accommodation in Tsuchiura itself is limited; staying in nearby Mito or Tsukuba and commuting in is a common approach. Tickets for reserved viewing areas are sold via lottery; free viewing areas exist further from the launch site.

4. Suwa Lake Fireworks, Nagano — The Most Photogenic Setting in Japan#

Date 2026: August 15 (Saturday) Location: Lake Suwa, Suwa City, Nagano Prefecture Scale: 40,000 shells in 75 minutes

Suwa differs from the competition festivals in character: this is not a competition but a pure display, and its setting — a mountain lake reflecting the explosions across still water, with the Alps visible on clear nights as a dark silhouette behind the smoke — makes it perhaps the most visually spectacular of Japan’s major fireworks events.

The scale is also startling: 40,000 shells in 75 minutes means an average of nearly nine shells per second at peak moments, the sound rolling back from the surrounding mountains in waves. The lake reflection doubles every explosion — what you see overhead is simultaneously happening in the water below. For photographers, Suwa on a clear August night is extraordinary.

The festival atmosphere around Lake Suwa on this evening is dense with summer energy: the lakeside promenade fills with yukata-dressed crowds from early afternoon, every yatai slot along the shore is taken by noon, and the surrounding inns (Suwa has a long hot spring tradition) fill with visitors who plan to soak in an onsen after the fireworks — one of the finer ways to end a summer evening in Japan.

Practical notes: Suwa is accessible from Shinjuku on the JR Chuo Line Limited Express in about two hours. Accommodation in Suwa and the surrounding towns should be booked months in advance for August 15. The lakeside viewing areas are free, but prime spots on the promenade require arriving very early — late morning at the latest for a front-row position. The return train is extremely crowded; consider staying overnight rather than attempting the same-night return to Tokyo.

What to Wear, Eat, and Bring#

Yukata: Renting a yukata is one of the more rewarding things you can do at a Japanese fireworks festival. Rental shops in most Japanese cities, usually near train stations, offer the full outfit including obi (sash) and wooden sandals, with staff who will dress you correctly. Prices typically run ¥3,000–¥6,000 for the evening. Walking through the yatai crowd in yukata shifts your experience of the festival — you become part of the spectacle rather than an observer of it, and Japanese festival-goers will often acknowledge you warmly for the effort.

Food: The yatai circuit is a festival in itself. Standard offerings include yakitori (grilled chicken skewers), takoyaki (octopus balls), yakisoba (grilled noodles), karaage (fried chicken), kakigori (shaved ice in flavours from matcha to strawberry), chocolate-dipped bananas, and grilled corn. Regional specialties appear at each festival — Akita sake at Omagari, Niigata rice products at Nagaoka, Suwa miso at the lakeside stalls. Budget ¥2,000–¥3,000 for an evening of yatai eating and you’ll be well fed.

What to bring: A picnic sheet or small folding mat for free viewing areas, cash (yatai vendors rarely take cards), insect repellent for riverside festivals, a light jacket for evening cooling (even in August, riverside nights drop), and comfortable shoes that can handle grass, gravel, and occasional mud.

Tickets and Logistics#

All four festivals sell reserved viewing seats — which provide guaranteed position, often with chairs or bench seating — through lottery systems on their official websites. The lotteries typically open three to six months before the event. For international visitors, navigating Japanese lottery websites can be challenging; guided tours that include reserved seating and transportation are a practical alternative for Nagaoka and Omagari in particular.

Free viewing areas exist at all four festivals but require very early arrival — at Omagari and Nagaoka, competitive free-spot claiming begins before noon. The tradeoff between arriving hours early for a free spot versus paying for a reserved seat with guaranteed position is one of the central logistics decisions for each festival.

Whatever your approach: book accommodation first. The hotels fill before the tickets.
