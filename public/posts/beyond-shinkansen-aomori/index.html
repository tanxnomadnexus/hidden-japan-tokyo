Beyond the Shinkansen Stop: Aomori

Most travelers pass straight through Shin-Aomori without stopping. Here's why that's a mistake — and what's waiting if you get off the train.
July 14, 2026 | Translations:
  • Ko
  • Zh-Tw

Most people on the Hokkaido Shinkansen treat Shin-Aomori the same way: they glance out the window, maybe notice the snow, and stay in their seat. The train continues north toward Hokkaido, or they reverse back south toward Tokyo, and Aomori gets filed under “not this trip.”

I understand the logic. Aomori doesn’t have a famous castle. It doesn’t have a row of Michelin-starred restaurants. The guidebooks mention it briefly — usually just the Nebuta Festival in August — and move on. So most foreign visitors move on too.

That’s their loss.

Aomori is the kind of place that takes about four hours to understand and then makes you wish you’d booked three nights instead of one. Here’s what’s actually there.

First, the basics#

Shin-Aomori is the shinkansen station. Aomori Station, the actual city center, is one stop further on a local train — about five minutes, ¥200. Don’t confuse them. I say this because I once watched a very confused group of European tourists stand on the Shin-Aomori platform for twenty minutes trying to figure out why the “city” they’d read about appeared to be a single building surrounded by rice paddies.

From Tokyo: about 3 hours 10 minutes on the Hayabusa shinkansen. From Sendai: about 1 hour 40 minutes. A Japan Rail Pass covers the full journey.

Once you’re in Aomori City, you need very little: most of what matters is within walking distance of Aomori Station, and what isn’t is accessible by bus or a short taxi ride.

The thing you must do on your first morning: Nokkedon at Furukawa Market#

Five minutes’ walk from Aomori Station is the Furukawa Fish Market, and inside it is one of the best food experiences in all of Tohoku. It’s called Nokkedon — “rice bowl with toppings” — and the concept is simple enough that it takes thirty seconds to explain and about three visits to fully appreciate.

You walk to the information desk and buy a book of tickets: ¥1,500 for ten tickets, ¥750 for five. One ticket gets you a bowl of rice from any stall flying an orange flag. Then you wander the market — around 25 vendors, all selling fresh seafood from Mutsu Bay and the surrounding waters — and exchange your remaining tickets for toppings. Two tickets for a slice of fatty tuna. Two for scallop. Three if you want sea urchin. The vendors will pile it directly onto your rice.

The result is a custom seafood bowl that costs around ¥2,000 and is fresher than almost anything you’ll eat in Tokyo, because the fish here traveled about forty minutes from the sea rather than several hours in a truck.

A few things nobody tells you: go early (the best tuna sells out by midday). Buy the larger ticket book because you will want more than you think. The ladies behind the counters speak Tsugaru dialect, not standard Japanese, and definitely not English — but they’ve been doing this long enough to understand “two of that one, please” in any language.

The market is open from around 7am. If you’re staying nearby, this is your breakfast plan sorted.

Nebuta Museum WA RASSE — even if you’re not here in August#

The Nebuta Festival happens in early August and draws roughly three million people over six days. The floats — enormous illuminated structures of samurai, demons, and mythological figures, some three stories tall — are made by hand over months, paraded through the city at night, and then retired.

Except some of them end up in WA RASSE, a museum ten minutes’ walk from Aomori Station, right on the waterfront. It displays four or five full-sized Nebuta from recent competitions, lit up in a darkened hall, with explanations of how they’re constructed. There’s something strange and arresting about encountering them outside of the festival context — just standing in a room with these massive glowing things, not a crowd in sight.

Entry: ¥620 adults. Hours: generally 9am–6pm (check ahead in winter). If you’re here in August for the actual festival, reserve accommodation months in advance and expect the city to be completely transformed.

Sukayu Onsen — one of the strangest baths in Japan#

About 70 minutes by bus from Aomori City (the bus runs from Aomori Station), Sukayu Onsen sits in the Hakkoda Mountains and claims to be the snowiest inhabited place on Earth. In a heavy winter, the snow around it can reach ten meters deep. The road there in January requires some faith.

The bath itself, called Sen-nin-buro (“Bath of One Thousand Bathers”), is one of the most unusual hot spring experiences in Japan: a massive wooden hall, beams black with age, the size of a small gymnasium, with multiple pools of different temperatures fed by natural springs. It’s traditionally mixed-gender — bathing suits for rental are available if you need them — which surprises foreign visitors more than Japanese ones.

The water smells strongly of sulfur. The wood has been absorbing steam for over a century. The whole place feels genuinely old in a way that newer onsen facilities, however elegant, never quite manage.

A day trip works: take the morning bus from Aomori Station, soak for two or three hours, eat lunch at the simple restaurant on-site, take the afternoon bus back. Bus fare: around ¥1,500 each way. Entry to the bath: ¥1,000.

If you want to stay overnight, Sukayu Onsen Ryokan is the only option — room and two meals included, book well in advance.

Hirosaki: the apple city with Japan’s most famous cherry blossoms#

An hour by local train from Aomori City (around ¥680), Hirosaki is technically a separate city but makes an obvious day trip. It’s the apple capital of Japan — Aomori Prefecture produces roughly 60% of the country’s apples, and Hirosaki takes particular pride in this — and in autumn the orchards around the city are worth a detour in themselves.

But most people come for the castle. Hirosaki Castle, surrounded by a moat and sitting inside a park of around 2,600 cherry trees, is consistently ranked among Japan’s top cherry blossom viewing spots. In late April and early May, the combination of the castle tower reflected in the moat and the overhanging cherry branches produces the kind of photograph you’ve probably seen without knowing where it was taken.

Outside cherry blossom season, Hirosaki is quieter and arguably more pleasant: the castle park is still beautiful, the old merchant district in the Nakamachi area preserves some interesting Meiji-era architecture, and the apple-themed food (apple pie, apple cider, apple curry — yes, really) is worth exploring.

Getting there: JR Ou Line from Aomori Station to Hirosaki, about 55 minutes.

The Winter Stove Train — if you’re visiting between late November and March#

This requires some planning but rewards it. JR East operates a series of “Stove Train” services on the Tsugaru Railway between late November and early March — old diesel cars heated by actual iron stoves, the kind you’d expect to find in a 1940s farmhouse, radiating warmth in the middle of the car while snow accumulates against the windows outside.

The train runs between Tsugaru-Goshogawara and Tsugaru-Nakasato, a journey of about 40 minutes. Vendors walk the car selling grilled squid (ikayaki) that you can cook directly on the stove top. The whole thing feels slightly improbable — this level of charm, on a working local train, for a few hundred yen.

Access: Take a local train from Aomori Station to Goshogawara (around 50 minutes on the JR Ou Line), then transfer to the Tsugaru Railway. The stove trains run a few times daily; check the schedule in advance.

What to eat beyond the market#

Aomori’s food culture goes deeper than Nokkedon, though that’s where most visitors stop.

Jappa-jiru is a winter cod hotpot — the whole fish, organs included, simmered with vegetables and tofu in miso broth. It’s the kind of dish that exists to keep people warm during Aomori winters, which are genuinely serious. You’ll find it at izakayas and small restaurants around the city from November to March. If you’re visiting in winter and you see it on a menu, order it.

Aomori scallops from Mutsu Bay are considered some of the best in Japan — sweeter and larger than average. They appear on the Nokkedon counter, on sushi menus, and grilled over charcoal at festival stalls.

Ringo-juice (apple juice) from local producers is available everywhere and ranges from cloudy, almost cider-like versions to smooth refined ones. The convenience stores here stock local brands you won’t find in Tokyo.

A practical 2-night itinerary#

Day 1 — Morning to Evening

  • Arrive Aomori Station by shinkansen
  • Walk to Furukawa Market for Nokkedon breakfast (7–10am)
  • Nebuta Museum WA RASSE (10am–noon)
  • Afternoon: walk the waterfront, explore A-FACTORY (local products store near the station)
  • Evening: izakaya dinner — look for Jappa-jiru if it’s winter, grilled Mutsu scallops any time of year

Day 2 — Sukayu

  • Morning bus to Sukayu Onsen (departs Aomori Station ~8:40am)
  • 2–3 hours soaking, lunch at the onsen restaurant
  • Afternoon bus back to Aomori
  • Evening at leisure — try the ramen shops near the station

Day 3 — Hirosaki (or depart)

  • Morning train to Hirosaki (55 minutes)
  • Hirosaki Castle park and surrounding district
  • Return to Aomori for shinkansen south, or continue north to Hokkaido

Approximate costs for 2 nights:

  • Business hotel near Aomori Station: ¥8,000–12,000/night
  • Nokkedon breakfast: ¥1,500–2,000
  • Sukayu day trip (bus + entry): around ¥4,000
  • Hirosaki day trip (train + entry): around ¥2,000
  • Food/drink budget: ¥3,000–5,000/day

Total for 2 nights, doing most of the above: roughly ¥40,000–55,000, not including the shinkansen from Tokyo.

When to go#

Late April to early May: Cherry blossoms in Hirosaki. Book accommodation months ahead — this is the one time when the region gets genuinely busy.

Early August: Nebuta Festival. The city transforms. Spectacular but crowded; book everything a year in advance if possible.

October to November: Autumn foliage in the Hakkoda Mountains and around Hirosaki. Clear skies and cool temperatures. One of the best times to visit.

December to March: Snow country. Sukayu at its most dramatic, the Stove Train running, Jappa-jiru on every menu. Cold — proper cold, not Tokyo-in-January cold — but rewarding for travelers who don’t mind dressing for it.

The shinkansen will keep running north. You don’t have to get off.

But if you do, and you give Aomori two or three days rather than a morning’s glance from a train window, you’ll end up in a city that feeds you extraordinarily well, puts you in some of the most atmospheric hot springs in Japan, and asks almost nothing in return except a willingness to figure things out without much English signage to help.

That, in the end, is what this series is about.
