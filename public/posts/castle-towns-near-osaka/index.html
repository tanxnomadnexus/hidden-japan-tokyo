Hidden Castle Towns Near Osaka: 5 Day Trips Beyond the Tourist Trail

Five historic castle towns within day-trip distance of Osaka that most foreign visitors never find.
June 27, 2026 | Translations:
  • Ko
  • Zh-Tw

Osaka’s tourist infrastructure is so well-developed that it can feel like the city is trying to keep you inside it — there’s always another food street, another neighborhood, another castle to see (its own castle being the obvious one). But the Kansai region around Osaka is one of the most historically layered parts of Japan, and within two or three hours in any direction lie castle towns that saw more history than most countries manage in a millennium.

These five are the ones that most foreign visitors miss entirely.

1. Sasayama (Tanba-Sasayama), Hyogo — A Perfectly Preserved Castle Town, 50 Minutes Away#

Sasayama — officially renamed Tanba-Sasayama in 2006, though locals still use the old name — is arguably the best-preserved castle town in the Kansai region, and the fact that almost no foreign tourists find their way here is one of those pleasant mysteries of Japanese travel. The town was built in 1609 by Tokugawa Ieyasu as a strategic base, and its street layout, merchant quarter, and samurai residential district survive in remarkably intact form.

The Okachimachi samurai district in particular is worth an unhurried morning: wide earthen-walled lanes, preserved gatehouses, and a quiet that’s hard to find anywhere this close to a major city. The town is also famous for Tanba black beans and boar (inoshishi) cuisine — both appear on menus throughout the old town.

Getting there: From Osaka’s Fukushima Station, take the JR Fukuchiyama Line (Takarazuka Line) to Sasayamaguchi Station. About 50 minutes. The castle town is a short bus ride or taxi from the station.

Don’t miss: The reconstructed Sasayama Castle (Ōshiro Rekishi-kan) and the Kasagake-no-Michi walking route through the samurai district.

Best season: Spring for cherry blossoms along the castle moat; autumn for the surrounding hills turning red and gold.

2. Takeda, Hyogo — The Castle in the Clouds#

Takeda Castle is not a reconstructed keep — it’s a ruin, which makes it far more atmospheric than most. Set on a mountaintop at 353 meters, the stone walls and foundation platforms remain largely intact, and when autumn fog fills the valley below, the castle appears to float above the clouds — a view that’s become famous in Japan and has still not reached most international guidebooks.

The castle was built in the 15th century and abandoned in the early 17th, leaving the stone platforms to weather for four centuries. Getting there requires a hike (about 40 minutes up the mountain), which is part of why it remains uncommercial. The town of Takeda at the base has good soba restaurants and a quiet, time-stopped quality.

Getting there: From Osaka Station, take the JR Bantan Line Limited Express (Hamakaze) to Takeda (Hyogo) Station. About 80 minutes. The mountain trail begins a short walk from the station.

Don’t miss: Arriving before 8am on a clear autumn morning for the best chance of seeing the sea of clouds below the ruins.

Best season: Late September to early November for fog and autumn color. Summer is clear but hot on the exposed mountain.

3. Iga, Mie — The Real Ninja Town#

Iga’s claim to fame is that it was historically the home of the Iga-ryu ninja school — one of the two most famous ninja traditions in Japan — and unlike most ninja-themed tourism, the connection here is genuine. The Iga-Ueno Castle and its surrounding town developed in parallel with the ninja clans that used the surrounding mountains as training and operating grounds for centuries.

The Iga-ryu Ninja Museum is better than it sounds — it goes well beyond costumes and throwing stars into actual history, espionage techniques, and the social structure of the ninja clans. The castle keep itself, rebuilt in the 1930s using traditional methods, has one of the highest stone bases of any Japanese castle. The old town around it has sake breweries, craft workshops, and a preserved merchant district that sees almost no foreign visitors.

Getting there: From Osaka Uehommachi Station, take the Kintetsu Osaka Line to Tsuruhashi, transfer to the Kintetsu Osaka Line Limited Express to Iga-Ueno (via Nabari). About 75 minutes total.

Don’t miss: The Iga-ryu Ninja Museum ninja demonstration (held several times daily) — more informative than theatrical.

Best season: Spring (cherry blossoms around the castle) and autumn. The town holds a ninja festival in late April.

4. Maruoka, Fukui — Japan’s Oldest Castle Keep#

Maruoka Castle has a claim that no other castle in Japan can make: its keep is the oldest surviving original castle tower in the country, built in 1576. Not reconstructed. Not restored beyond recognition. The original wood and stone, standing for 450 years in the quiet countryside of Fukui Prefecture. It’s small — three stories, simply designed — but the age of the timber inside is palpable in a way that gleaming reconstructions never achieve.

Fukui Prefecture as a whole is one of Japan’s least-visited, and Maruoka sits in flat agricultural land surrounded by rice fields. The contrast between the ancient keep and the ordinary rural landscape around it gives the place an understated, unperformed quality. The castle is known in Japan for a cherry blossom viewing festival each spring, when hundreds of trees around the moat come into bloom.

Getting there: From Osaka Station, take the Thunderbird Limited Express to Fukui Station, then a local bus to Maruoka. About 2 hours total.

Don’t miss: The castle keep interior, which you can climb on a narrow original staircase — almost vertical, as was common in the period. Hold the rope.

Best season: Late March to early April for cherry blossoms; the castle itself is worth the visit in any season.

5. Bitchu-Matsuyama, Okayama — Japan’s Highest Castle, Almost Nobody Visits#

Bitchu-Matsuyama Castle sits at 430 meters — the highest elevation of any castle keep in Japan — and it is, like Maruoka, an original structure rather than a reconstruction. Built in the 13th century and expanded in the 17th, the two-story keep clings to a granite peak in the mountains of Okayama Prefecture, approached by a hiking trail through cedar forest.

On autumn mornings, it shares the sea-of-clouds phenomenon with Takeda Castle — the castle rising above fog in the valley, the mountains emerging beyond. The town of Takahashi at the base of the mountain has one of the best-preserved samurai district streetscapes in western Japan, with clay-walled residences lining lanes that have changed very little since the Edo period.

Getting there: From Osaka Station, take the Shinkansen to Okayama, then the JR Hakubi Line to Bitchu-Takahashi Station. About 2 hours total. The castle trail begins about 20 minutes on foot from the station.

Don’t miss: The Raikyu-ji garden at the base of the mountain, a nationally designated scenic garden designed in the early Edo period with the castle visible above the tree line.

Best season: Late September to early November for autumn fog and foliage. Spring for cherry blossoms along the mountain trail.

Planning Your Visit#

All five are reachable as day trips from Osaka, though Maruoka and Bitchu-Matsuyama are at the outer edge of comfortable day-trip range — an overnight stay in Fukui or Okayama makes them more relaxed. A Japan Rail Pass covers the Shinkansen and JR lines to all five; the Kintetsu network to Iga requires a separate ticket (affordable).

The less-visited a castle town, the more likely the local tourism infrastructure is in Japanese only — bring Google Translate’s camera mode and embrace the occasional uncertainty. The reward, in every case, is a version of Japan that most visitors to Osaka never suspect exists an hour or two down the line.
