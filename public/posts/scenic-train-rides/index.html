Japan's Most Scenic Local Train Rides Away from the Crowds

Discover Japan's most beautiful local train journeys that most foreign visitors never find.
June 25, 2026 | Translations:
  • Ko
  • Zh-Tw

Here’s something the big travel guides don’t tell you: the Shinkansen is efficient, yes, but it’s also mostly underground or elevated — you spend half the journey staring at the inside of a tunnel or the back of someone’s seat. The real Japan, the one with rice paddies going gold in October and fishing villages clinging to cliffsides and rivers you can see the bottom of, reveals itself on the slow trains. The ones that rattle. The ones where someone might get on carrying a box of vegetables.

I’ve ridden a lot of Japanese trains. The five I’m about to share aren’t just scenic train rides in Japan — they’re the kind of journeys where you find yourself leaning against the window and forgetting entirely where you were supposed to be going.

1. Akechi Railway Akechi Line — A Scenic Local Train Japan Ride Through the Tōnō Highlands#

Gifu Prefecture doesn’t get nearly enough credit, and the Akechi Line is part of why that should change. This small third-sector railway runs 25 kilometers between Ena and Akechi through the Higashi-Mino highlands — a landscape of forested ridges, persimmon orchards, and farmhouses with thatched roofs that look unchanged since the Meiji era. The train itself is a single diesel car, often with just a handful of passengers, and it winds through mountain terrain at a pace that feels almost apologetic.

The centerpiece is the Ena Gorge section near the start of the line, where the train crosses elevated bridges over the Kiso River. In autumn the gorge ignites in reds and oranges. In spring, cherry trees line the embankments around Iwamura Station.

Board at: Ena Station (accessible from Nagoya on the JR Chūō Line in about an hour).

Don’t miss: Iwamura, a beautifully preserved castle town midway along the line. The stone-paved merchant street and hilltop castle ruins are worth a few hours.

Best season: Late October to early November for autumn foliage; late March to early April for cherry blossoms along the tracks.

2. Sanriku Railway Rias Line — Scenic Train Rides Japan’s Rugged Pacific Coast#

The Sanriku Coast in Iwate Prefecture is one of Japan’s most dramatic and, given its history with the 2011 tsunami, most quietly moving landscapes. The Rias Line runs 163 kilometers between Sakari and Kuji, threading through deep rias inlets, sea cliffs, and fishing villages perched above coves of deep blue water. On clear days the Pacific stretches to the horizon; on foggy mornings the coastline appears and disappears like something from a dream.

This is one of Japan’s longer local train rides, and it rewards those who take their time. Several trains operate as “resort” services with panoramic windows, but the standard local trains are just as good — and often half empty. The line passes through areas that were devastated in 2011 and rebuilt, and there’s a dignified resilience to the communities along it that you feel without anyone having to say a word.

Board at: Sakari Station (from Ichinoseki via JR Ofunato Line) or Kamaishi Station, which is more central.

Don’t miss: Jodogahama Beach near Miyako Station — white pebbles, turquoise water, dramatic sea stacks. Take the 15-minute bus from the station.

Best season: May through September for clear skies and calm seas. Winter is atmospheric but some services are reduced.

3. Yotsukura–Hama-Kōgen Stretch of the JR Jōban Line — Japan’s Local Train Ride with an Ocean on One Side and Mountains on the Other#

Most people use the Jōban Line as a commuter route through Fukushima and Ibaraki. Skip the urban section and join at Yotsukura heading south toward Iwaki, and you’ll find a stretch where the Pacific Ocean sits so close to the tracks you can see the surf from your seat — then, within minutes, the terrain shifts to forested hills and small farming valleys. It’s an unusually varied journey for a relatively short ride.

This isn’t a famous scenic route, which is precisely the point. You’ll be on a regular local train with fishermen and schoolkids and older women carrying shopping bags. The scenery, particularly around Onahama and the coast approaching Nakoso, is genuinely beautiful — seawall murals, rocky beaches, lighthouse promontories — and almost completely off the foreign tourist radar.

Board at: Iwaki Station (reachable from Tokyo’s Ueno Station on the Jōban Line Limited Express in about 2 hours).

Don’t miss: Aquamarine Fukushima, one of Japan’s best aquariums, a short ride from Onahama Station. Also worth exploring: the cliffside Shiramizu Amidadō temple near Heianjo Station, a National Treasure rarely visited by tourists.

Best season: Spring (April–May) and autumn (October). Summer mornings offer stunning sea mist on the coastal stretches.

4. Uchiko Line and JR Yodo River Valley — Scenic Local Trains Through Shikoku’s Hidden Interior#

Shikoku is already Japan’s least-visited main island, but most travelers who do make it there stick to the coastal routes and pilgrimage towns. Head inland from Matsuyama on the JR Yosan Line toward Uchiko, then continue on the Uchiko Line into the Yodo River valley, and you’ll find yourself in a landscape that feels genuinely remote — cedar forests, terraced rice fields, small river settlements, mountain peaks in every direction.

The Uchiko Line section is only 12 kilometers, but it’s one of the loveliest short runs in Japan, crossing the Oda River on an old iron bridge and passing through villages where the station platforms double as community bulletin boards. Uchiko town itself is a gem: a preserved merchant quarter called Yokaichi-Gokoku with white-walled warehouses and a working kabuki theater.

Board at: Matsuyama Station. The express to Uchiko takes about 45 minutes; local trains take longer and see more.

Don’t miss: Uchiko-za, a beautifully restored Taishō-era kabuki theater open for tours. The Uchiko wax museum is also unexpectedly fascinating — the town built its prosperity on Japanese wax in the 19th century.

Best season: Late April for new green on the cedar hills; October and November for autumn color along the river valley.

5. Hisatsu Orange Railway — A Scenic Local Train Japan Ride Between Sea Cliffs and the East China Sea#

The Hisatsu Orange Railway runs 116 kilometers down the western coast of Kyushu between Sendai (Kagoshima) and Yatsushiro (Kumamoto), and it might be the most visually spectacular local train ride in the entire country. The line clings to cliffsides above the East China Sea for long stretches, passing fishing ports, sea caves, and subtropical headlands with views that have absolutely no business being on a 500-yen local train ticket.

The railway was taken over from JR Kyushu by local authorities, and they’ve made a real effort to make it special — some services use restored old carriages, and the stations are small, proud, and full of local character. The town of Akune, roughly in the middle, is known for its kumquat orchards and a coastline that looks more like southern Italy than Japan.

Board at: Sendai Station in Kagoshima Prefecture (not to be confused with Sendai in Tohoku — this is a different city). Reach it via the Shinkansen to Kagoshima-Chūō, then JR Kagoshima Main Line.

Don’t miss: Izumi Station — from here you can reach the Izumi Crane Reserve, where over 10,000 Siberian cranes winter between November and March. An extraordinary spectacle if your timing aligns.

Best season: Autumn and winter for the crane season; late spring (May) for the sea in full color under clear Kyushu skies.

Final Thoughts#

The great thing about Japan’s local train network is that it’s reliable, affordable, and almost entirely ignored by foreign tourists once you step off the main Shinkansen corridors. A Japan Rail Pass covers most of the JR lines on this list; the third-sector railways (Akechi, Sanriku, Hisatsu Orange) charge separately but all are very reasonably priced.

None of these journeys require advance booking. Just show up, buy a ticket from the machine, and sit on the side with the view. You might be the only foreigner on the train. The people around you will occasionally glance over, notice you’re paying attention to the landscape, and feel quietly pleased that someone from elsewhere has found their way here.

That feeling, on both sides of it, is worth more than any famous sight.

Practical tip: IC cards (Suica, ICOCA) work on most JR lines. For third-sector railways, have cash ready. Google Maps is surprisingly good at local train schedules in Japan — use it to time your connections.
