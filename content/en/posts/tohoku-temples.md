Kyoto gets all the attention when people talk about temples in Japan, and fair enough — it's spectacular. But it's also wall-to-wall tour groups, timed entry slots, and selfie sticks blocking your view of a thousand-year-old gate. Head north into Tohoku, though, and you'll find some of the country's most atmospheric, historically significant sites with a fraction of the crowd, sometimes none at all.

Tohoku — the six northern prefectures of Aomori, Akita, Iwate, Miyagi, Yamagata, and Fukushima — doesn't show up on most first-time itineraries, which is exactly why it's worth the extra train ride. Below are five hidden temples Japan rarely puts on the cover of a guidebook, the kind of Tohoku shrines and temples where you might find yourself alone on a stone staircase, listening to nothing but wind and cedar trees. Here's where to go and how to get there.

1. Yamadera (Risshaku-ji), Yamagata

Officially called Risshaku-ji, Yamadera literally means "mountain temple," and it earns the name. Founded in 860 by the priest Jikaku Daishi, this Tendai Buddhist complex is built directly into a cliffside, with various halls and shrines scattered along a route of over 1,000 stone steps. The 17th-century poet Matsuo Basho climbed this same path and was moved to write one of his most famous haiku about the stillness of the place. Reach the top and you're rewarded with sweeping views over the Yamagata valley from the Godaido Hall — one of the best panoramas in northern Japan.

Getting there: A 5-minute walk from JR Yamadera Station, easily reached by local train from Yamagata City or as a stop on the Sendai–Yamagata rail route.

Nearby: Yamagata City itself has hot springs, including the famous Zao Onsen, plus the dramatic Zao volcanic crater if you have an extra day.

Best season: Late spring (May) for lush greenery and quieter crowds, or autumn for foliage — though autumn weekends do draw more visitors than the rest of the year.

2. Chusonji Temple, Hiraizumi, Iwate

Hiraizumi was once a political and cultural rival to Kyoto itself, a fact that surprises most travelers who've never heard of it. Chusonji, founded in the 9th century by the same priest who established Yamadera, expanded dramatically in the 12th century under the Northern Fujiwara clan, who briefly made this remote town one of the most powerful places in Japan. Its centerpiece, the Konjikido (Golden Hall), is covered entirely in gold leaf, mother-of-pearl inlay, and lacquer — a startling sight tucked away in the quiet mountains of Iwate. The whole site was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2011.

Getting there: A 10-minute walk (or short bus ride) from JR Hiraizumi Station, accessible via the JR Tohoku Line with a transfer in Ichinoseki.

Nearby: Motsuji Temple, just down the road, has a beautifully preserved Heian-era garden. Geibikei Gorge offers a peaceful boat ride poled along by a singing oarsman.

Best season: Autumn, when the temple's golden hall is framed by surrounding maple color, or winter for a hushed, snow-covered atmosphere.

3. Zuiganji Temple, Matsushima, Miyagi

Matsushima Bay is famous for its scattering of pine-covered islands, considered one of Japan's three most scenic views, but most visitors snap a few photos from the boat and move on without ever stepping into Zuiganji. Founded in 828 by Jikaku Daishi — yes, the same priest behind both Yamadera and Chusonji — this Rinzai Zen temple belongs to the Myoshin-ji school based in Kyoto and carries centuries of patronage from the powerful Date clan, who once ruled this region. The wooden main hall and surrounding cave shrines, carved directly into the cliffside, have a weathered, contemplative quality that's easy to miss if you're rushing between bay viewpoints.

Getting there: About a 15-minute walk from Matsushima-Kaigan Station, or a 7-minute walk from Hon-Shiogama Station, both reachable from Sendai in roughly 20–25 minutes by local train.

Nearby: Toro Entsuin, just behind Zuiganji, has a quietly beautiful rose garden. Shiogama Jinja, the head shrine of hundreds of Shiogama shrines nationwide, is a short trip away and is also known for excellent sushi in the surrounding port town.

Best season: Year-round, though early autumn pairs nicely with mild weather for exploring the bay by boat beforehand.

4. Dewa Sanzan Shrines, Yamagata

Dewa Sanzan — the "Three Mountains of Dewa" — has been one of Japan's most important pilgrimage destinations for over 1,400 years, home to a unique mountain ascetic tradition known as Shugendo, practiced by Yamabushi monks who train here in extreme conditions. Each of the three sacred peaks (Haguro, Gassan, and Yudono) represents a stage of life: birth, death, and rebirth. Climbing the 2,446 stone steps up Mount Haguro to reach Dewa Sanzan Shrine is a genuine pilgrimage experience, not a curated tourist activity, and you'll likely share the path with actual practitioners rather than fellow sightseers.

Getting there: From Tsuruoka Station, take a local bus toward Haguro-san, then continue on foot up the mountain trail; the climb itself takes a few hours at a comfortable pace.

Nearby: Tsuruoka's old town has excellent shojin ryori (Buddhist mountain cuisine) restaurants, many run by families connected to the Yamabushi tradition.

Best season: Summer, when all three peaks are accessible (Gassan and Yudono close in winter due to snow), though Haguro itself can be visited year-round.

5. Shiogama Jinja, Miyagi

Easy to overlook in favor of its more famous neighbor Matsushima, Shiogama Jinja is actually the head shrine of several hundred Shiogama shrines scattered across Japan, making it a far more significant site than its modest crowds suggest. Set on a forested hill above the working port town of Shiogama, the shrine's weathered wooden architecture and quiet stone pathways offer a genuine sense of place rarely found at more heavily promoted shrines. The walk up through the trees, away from the bustle of the fish market below, feels like stepping backward in time by several centuries.

Getting there: A 15-minute walk from Shiogama Station, or a 7-minute walk from Hon-Shiogama Station, both about 20–25 minutes from Sendai Station by train.

Nearby: Shiogama's port area is one of the best places in the region for fresh sushi, with several respected shops near the harbor. Zuiganji and Matsushima Bay are a short ride away for a combined day trip.

Best season: Spring and autumn offer the most comfortable weather for the hillside walk, though the shrine's atmosphere holds up in any season.

Final thoughts

None of these five sites will pull the crowds that Kiyomizu-dera or Fushimi Inari draw every single day, and that's the whole point. Tohoku rewards travelers who are willing to take a local train an extra hour north, who don't mind handwritten signs instead of multilingual placards, and who'd rather have a centuries-old hall mostly to themselves than fight for a photo angle.

If you only have time for one stop, let your interests guide you: Yamadera if you want drama and a view, Chusonji if history and gold leaf are your thing, Dewa Sanzan if you want something closer to a genuine spiritual journey than a sightseeing stop. However you choose, these hidden temples Japan keeps tucked away in its northern mountains, and the quieter Tohoku shrines surrounding them, are some of the most rewarding stops you can make off the standard tourist trail.
