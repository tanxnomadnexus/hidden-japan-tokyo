Tokyo is an extraordinary city, but it has a gravitational pull that keeps most visitors locked within its orbit — or sends them shooting directly to Kyoto on the Shinkansen without stopping. What they miss is a ring of historic castle towns within two or three hours of the city, places where feudal Japan left its mark in stone walls, samurai districts, and merchant streets that survived the 20th century more or less intact.

These aren't on the standard day-trip circuit. You won't find them on most "things to do near Tokyo" lists. That's the point.

---

## 1. Kawagoe, Saitama — "Little Edo," 30 Minutes from Ikebukuro

Kawagoe is the closest thing to a time capsule in the Tokyo metropolitan area. Its Kurazukuri district — a street of heavy black-and-white clay-walled merchant storehouses — was built after a fire destroyed the town in 1893, and it survived the 20th century largely untouched. Walking through it feels genuinely different from anywhere else near Tokyo: the buildings are solid, old, and built to last in a way that modern Japanese architecture rarely is.

The town was a major commercial center during the Edo period, and its street plan still reflects that — narrow alleys behind the main street lead to small shrines, traditional sweet shops, and craft workshops. The five-story bell tower (Toki no Kane) has rung on the hour since the 17th century. The sweet potato is the local specialty, appearing in every form from chips to ice cream to sake.

**Getting there:** From Ikebukuro Station, take the Tobu Tojo Line Express to Kawagoe Station. About 30 minutes, no transfer needed.

**Don't miss:** Kita-in Temple, a short walk from the main street, has 540 stone figures of Buddha's disciples — each with a different expression — in a quiet garden that most visitors skip entirely.

**Best season:** October for autumn color in the temple gardens; the Kawagoe Festival in mid-October features elaborate festival floats and is one of the Kanto region's finest.

## 2. Ashikaga, Tochigi — Japan's Oldest School and a Forgotten Silk Town

Ashikaga was one of Japan's most powerful clans during the Muromachi period (they gave their name to the Ashikaga Shogunate), and the town they built along the Watarase River still has the bones of that history. The Ashikaga Gakko — the oldest school in Japan, founded in the 9th century — sits in the center of town, its grounds immaculately preserved as a historical site. A short walk away, Bannaji Temple occupies the former site of the Ashikaga clan's residence.

The town made its fortune in silk during the Meiji era, and the old textile district near the river has a quietly industrial beauty — brick warehouses, iron bridges, and the kind of functional architecture that tends to get demolished everywhere else. Ashikaga Flower Park, just outside town, draws Japanese visitors for its wisteria in late April and early May — tunnels of purple flowers that are genuinely spectacular.

**Getting there:** From Shinjuku Station, take the JR Ryomo Line Limited Express from Oyama to Ashikaga Station. About 90 minutes total.

**Don't miss:** The wisteria at Ashikaga Flower Park (late April to early May) — book ahead if visiting during peak bloom, as timed entry tickets sell out.

**Best season:** Late April to early May for wisteria; autumn for the river valley foliage.

## 3. Otaki, Chiba — A Forgotten Castle Town in the Boso Hills

Almost no foreign visitors make it to Otaki, which sits in the quiet hills of interior Chiba about 90 minutes from Tokyo. The castle here — a reconstructed keep on a forested hill above the Yoro River — is small by Japanese standards, but the surrounding town is one of the best-preserved castle town streetscapes in the Kanto region. Old merchant houses line the approach road, several still operating as sake breweries or traditional shops.

The Yoro River valley below the castle is excellent for a gentle walk, particularly in autumn when the hillsides turn. The whole area has a sleepy, forgotten quality that's genuinely hard to find this close to Tokyo — the kind of place where the station has no convenience store and the only sound in the castle grounds is birds.

**Getting there:** From Tokyo Station, take the JR Sobu Line to Chiba, then the Gakunan Railway to Goi, then the Kominato Railway to Otaki. Alternatively, a direct highway bus from Tokyo Station takes about 90 minutes.

**Don't miss:** The sake breweries along the old merchant street — several offer tastings and some have been operating since the Edo period.

**Best season:** Autumn (October–November) for the river valley foliage; spring for cherry blossoms on the castle hill.

## 4. Ueda, Nagano — The Castle That Defeated Tokugawa Twice

Ueda has one of the better castle stories in Japan: its lord, Sanada Masayuki, successfully defended this relatively small castle against Tokugawa Ieyasu's forces not once but twice — a feat that made the Sanada clan famous and that Japanese people still talk about with a kind of gleeful pride. The castle ruins that remain are atmospheric rather than grand, but the story they carry gives the stones a weight that reconstructed keeps elsewhere don't have.

The town itself sits in the Ueda Basin surrounded by mountains, with the Chikuma River running along its edge. The old castle town district has good restaurants, craft sake and miso producers, and the kind of provincial city energy that feels more genuinely Japanese than anything in a tourist hotspot. Bessho Onsen, a short bus ride away, is one of the oldest hot spring villages in Nagano.

**Getting there:** From Tokyo Station, take the Hokuriku Shinkansen (Kagayaki or Hakutaka) to Ueda Station. About 75 minutes.

**Don't miss:** The Sanada Museum in the castle park, which tells the clan's remarkable story. Bessho Onsen for a half-day addition — 30 minutes by local train from Ueda.

**Best season:** Spring for cherry blossoms in the castle park; autumn for mountain foliage around the basin.

## 5. Tsurugajo (Aizu-Wakamatsu), Fukushima — The White Castle of the North

Aizu-Wakamatsu is further than the others — about 2.5 hours from Tokyo — but it rewards the journey with one of the most atmospheric castle towns in eastern Japan. Tsurugajo, the reconstructed castle at its center, is known as the "white castle" for its distinctive pale walls, and it was the site of one of the final battles of the Boshin War in 1868, when a group of teenage samurai defenders (the Byakkotai, or White Tiger Brigade) became one of the most tragic stories of Japan's civil war.

The castle town around it preserves its Edo-period layout better than most: samurai residences, lacquerware workshops, sake breweries along the old canal, and a general sense that the 19th century wasn't that long ago. The local Aizu lacquerware and sake are excellent souvenirs, and the castle grounds in cherry blossom season are among the best in Tohoku.

**Getting there:** From Tokyo Station, take the Tohoku Shinkansen to Koriyama, then the Banetsu West Line to Aizu-Wakamatsu. About 2.5 hours total. A direct highway bus from Shinjuku also runs and takes about 3.5 hours.

**Don't miss:** Iimori Hill, a short bus ride from the castle, where the Byakkotai story is commemorated. Overlooking the castle from the hillside gives the best view of Tsurugajo in context.

**Best season:** Late April for cherry blossoms in the castle grounds; autumn for the surrounding mountain foliage.

## Planning Your Visit

All five towns are manageable as day trips from Tokyo, though Aizu-Wakamatsu and Ueda reward an overnight stay if your schedule allows. A Japan Rail Pass covers the Shinkansen legs to Ueda and Aizu-Wakamatsu, as well as the JR lines to Kawagoe and Ashikaga. For Otaki, check highway bus options from Tokyo Station — they're often faster and cheaper than the train combination.

None of these towns require advance booking for the main sights, though Ashikaga Flower Park during wisteria season is the exception: timed entry tickets sell out weeks in advance.
